How Many Hours Can Freelancers Actually Bill Per Week?

June 16, 2026 · 6 min read

When people imagine freelancing, they picture 40 hours of billable work, 52 weeks a year, at their full rate.

Reality looks different. And the gap between "hours worked" and "hours billed" is where most freelancers lose money without realizing it.

Let's look at the actual numbers — not what's possible in theory, but what real freelancers experience in practice.

1. The 25-Hour Truth

Across multiple freelance surveys and our own data, the average freelancer bills between 20 and 30 hours per week. We use 25 as the default in our calculator, which is on the optimistic side for most people.

Here's why you can't bill 40 hours:

Admin work is real. Every client engagement generates emails, invoices, contracts, follow-ups, and bookkeeping. That's not billable — but it's mandatory. Estimate 5-8 hours per week just for this.

Sales & marketing keep you alive. Even when you're busy, you need to maintain your pipeline. The moment you stop looking for the next client is the moment your income becomes fragile. That's another 3-5 hours per week.

Professional development isn't optional. Skills expire. Tools change. If you're not learning, you're falling behind. Budget 2-4 hours per week for courses, tutorials, and experimentation.

Your brain needs breaks. Deep work — the kind clients actually pay for — is mentally exhausting. Few people can sustain more than 5-6 hours of it per day. After that, the quality drops and mistakes creep in.

"If you're billing 35 hours a week consistently, you're either a superhero or you're burning out. Most people land at 20-25 sustainable billable hours."

That's a 62.5% utilization rate. If you're early in your freelance career, expect it to be lower — closer to 50%. If you're established with repeat clients and streamlined processes, you might hit 70-75%.

3. Your Utilization Rate

Your utilization rate is the percentage of your working hours that you can actually bill to clients. It's the single most important metric you're probably not tracking.

Here's why it matters:

If you work 40 hours a week but only bill 25, your effective hourly rate is not your stated rate. It's your stated rate × 0.625.

That's the real number. If your utilization is 50% — which is common for new freelancers — your $100/hour rate is actually earning you $50 per hour worked. You need to either raise your rate, improve your utilization, or both.

4. How to Improve Your Billable Hours

You can't eliminate non-billable work. But you can shrink it. Here's how:

Template everything. Proposals, contracts, invoices, follow-up emails — create templates for all of them. Every time you write something from scratch, you're burning billable hours. A library of 10 good templates can save you 3-4 hours a week.

Batch your admin. Don't check email 20 times a day. Batch it to two 30-minute blocks — once in the morning, once in the afternoon. You'll be amazed how much time this frees up.

Raise your rates to filter clients. Higher rates attract better clients who require less hand-holding. A $50/hour client might need 5 hours of communication per week. A $150/hour client typically needs 1-2. Better clients = better utilization.

Use retainers. Retainer clients require less sales overhead (you're not constantly selling) and less admin (one invoice, one contract per month). They're the single best way to stabilize your billable hours.

Track your time for two weeks. Before you make any changes, track every hour — billable and non-billable — for 14 days. You'll see exactly where your time goes. Most freelancers are shocked by how much time slips through the cracks.
