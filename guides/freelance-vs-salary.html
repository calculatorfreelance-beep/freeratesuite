Freelance vs Full-Time Salary: What's the Real Difference?

June 16, 2026 · 8 min read

"I make $70,000 at my job, so I need to earn $70,000 freelancing."

I hear this constantly. And it sounds logical. But it's dead wrong.

A $70,000 salary and $70,000 in freelance revenue are not the same thing. Not even close. One leaves you with money in the bank. The other leaves you wondering why you're working harder for less.

This article breaks down the real difference — not the emotional stuff about "being your own boss," but the actual dollar amounts.

1. The Hard Math

Let's say your full-time job pays $70,000 a year. Here's what that actually costs your employer — and what it would cost you as a freelancer to match it.

Read that bottom line again. To match the lifestyle of a $70,000 salaried job, you actually need to earn about $85,000+ in freelance revenue. The $22,000 gap is the "tax" of being self-employed — and it's real.

"A good rule of thumb: multiply your desired salary by 1.3 to get your freelance revenue target. If you want to live like you made $70K, you need to bill about $91K."

2. Hidden Costs Nobody Warns You About

Beyond the obvious line items, there are quieter costs that eat into freelance income:

Gap time between projects. Even successful freelancers average 2 to 4 weeks a year where they're actively looking for the next gig. That's unpaid time that your rate needs to absorb.

Non-billable work. Proposals, estimates, invoicing, follow-ups, bookkeeping. It adds up to 10-15 hours a week that generates zero revenue. If you're billing 25 hours, you're probably working 40.

Slow-paying clients. Net-30 often means Net-60 or Net-90. Meanwhile your bills are due on the 1st. The stress of cash flow gaps is a real cost, even if it doesn't show up on a spreadsheet.

Self-funded retirement. Your employer's 401k match is gone. You now have to save entirely on your own. A SEP IRA or Solo 401k helps, but the money comes from your pocket.

3. The Benefits Trade-Off

It's not all bad news. Freelancing has financial advantages that don't show up in a salary comparison:

  • Tax deductions. Your home office, internet, phone, software, hardware, a portion of your utilities, health insurance premiums — all deductible. An employee can't deduct any of that.
  • Uncapped income. Your salary has a ceiling. Your freelance rate doesn't. Raise it 10% every six months and you're earning 46% more in two years.
  • Geographic arbitrage. Work from a lower-cost city or country while charging rates based on a higher-cost market. That's pure profit.
  • Multiple income streams. You're not dependent on one employer. Lose one client, you've still got others. That diversification has real value.

These numbers might feel high compared to what you've been charging. That's the point. Most freelancers underprice themselves because they compare their rate to their old hourly wage — which didn't account for any of the costs we just covered.

Your rate isn't a reflection of your hourly "worth." It's a business calculation. Treat it like one.
